
Modeling and Traversing a Multimodal Dependency Hypergraph
Final Thesis
Your main thesis is fundamentally correct. The modern dependency management problem does not reduce to “DFS/BFS over a graph,” but rather to determining which graph exists at all under given resolver semantics, at a given time, for a given environment, and under a given set of constraints. HyperRes formalizes package resolution as a directed hypergraph capable of modeling many ecosystems and their translations; Package Managers à la Carte shows the common semantic core of dependency resolution and proves that the general problem is NP-complete; and the ACM Queue article demonstrates in practice that the same package may have many valid dependency graphs depending on the resolver, other dependencies in the project, the state of the registry, the platform, and time. In that sense, the statement “the hard part is not traversing the graph” is well supported by the literature. [1]
Two important corrections are still needed. First, not every “optional dependency” belongs to the same formal level: Package Managers à la Carte shows that some optional dependencies belong to build-graph semantics rather than the resolution core itself, so you must not lump everything together without preserving provenance and relation type. Second, some ecosystems deliberately restrict the semantics of the problem so that solving actually degrades to simpler traversal or greedy selection; formally, this happens, for example, after removing version uniqueness or after restricting constraints to lower bounds. This does not invalidate your thesis, but it refines it: the problem is not “always NP-hard,” but rather “NP-hard in general, but much simpler in important special classes.” [2]
Validation of the Cited Sources
The strongest support for your model does indeed come from HyperRes and Package Managers à la Carte. Solving Package Management via Hypergraph Dependency Resolution exists as a June 2025 paper and explicitly states that HyperRes is a hypergraph formalism “expressive enough to model many ecosystems,” with translations from “dozens of existing package managers,” and that it allows solving to be specialized to a specific deployment environment. Package Managers à la Carte, in turn, exists as a February 2026 preprint and builds “Package Calculus” as an intermediate representation for dependency expression, showing both formal reductions between ecosystems and the NP-completeness of the core problem as well as a reduction to SAT. That pair is currently the closest thing to what you described as a “versioned typed property hypergraph” with a constraint-solving layer. [3]
The ACM Queue article The Surprise of Multiple Dependency Graphs very accurately supports your argument that “a project does not have a single graph.” The authors show that dependency resolution is not deterministic in the practical sense: it is affected by other dependencies in the same project, version-selection policy, the current state of the registry, the tool used, the architecture, and the operating system. For example, they report that enumerating valid dependency graphs for webpack/5.94.0 yields approximately 6.2 × 10^42 possible graphs, and also that graphs in npm and PyPI change regularly over time as new versions are published. This is direct support for your thesis that “the graph” must be understood as a family of graphs dependent on context. [4]
deps.dev / Open Source Insights is a very good architectural benchmark, but not a complete answer to your problem. The official API separates requirements from resolved dependencies, returns resolved dependency graphs keyed by package version, and exposes dependents, advisories, licenses, and hash-based lookups. At the same time, the official documentation explicitly states that resolved graphs are computed as a variant close to a “generic 64-bit Linux system, with no other dependencies present,” so they are an excellent point of reference, but they do not represent the full space of contexts you are describing. In addition, full resolved dependency graphs are not currently equally available across all supported systems, which again shows that a production benchmark and a canonical semantic model are not the same thing. [5]
The section on reachability indexes is also well chosen, with one small bibliographic refinement. The survey Indexing Techniques for Graph Reachability Queries exists as an arXiv version developed from 2023 onward and as a publication in ACM Computing Surveys from December 2025; it classifies plain-graph and edge-labeled reachability indexes and organizes classes such as tree cover, 2-hop labeling, and approximate transitive closure. O’Reach is an important candidate for the query layer, but it is not a 2024 paper—its open conference version is from SEA 2021, and the later journal publication is from 2022. BL, by contrast, actually is a more recent example, published in 2024 in IEEE Transactions on Big Data as a reachability-indexing framework with a minBL variant. Your intuition of “not always traversing, but querying an index” is therefore correct, but a more precise dating of this part looks exactly like this. [6]
The section on storage and compression also has solid foundations. CSR remains the baseline format for fast scans and traversals on static snapshots; PCSR shows how to obtain much faster inserts at the cost of a constant loss in traversal performance and additional space overhead; LSMGraph goes a step further and combines a write-friendly LSM-tree with a read-friendly multi-level CSR; and ChunkGraph shows a sensible model for processing very large graphs on NVMe through a chunk-based representation, classified/hierarchical vertex storage, and layout optimization. DAG compression from STACS 2024 is theoretically very promising, but more as a second wave of optimization than as a project starting point. [7]
Finally, FASTEN strongly supports your intuition that a package-level dependency graph is not enough for security and impact analysis. The official FASTEN project describes itself as analyzing package dependency networks “at the call graph level,” and more recent work/theses from TU Delft show scalable call-graph generation for method-level vulnerability analysis and change impact analysis. This reinforces your conclusion that a mature system should contain a “package graph” layer as well as a higher “code reachability” layer, because “package present” and “vulnerable code actually reachable” are two different things. [8]
What This Problem Is Formally
The most precise description is not “dependency graph,” but rather: context-conditioned, versioned dependency-resolution problem over a typed hypergraph, followed by one or more resolved graph projections. HyperRes models a dependency as a hyperedge from one package to a set of packages, with labels for dependency, optional dependency, and conflict. This fits real ecosystems much better than an ordinary simple digraph, because one requirement may mean “choose exactly one of these candidates,” not “follow this one edge.” HyperRes also shows formalizations for virtual packages, computer architecture, and feature-parameterized dependencies. [9]
This formalization must additionally carry contextual predicates, because real packaging languages are conditional. In Python there are extras and environment markers, which may activate dependencies only for a specific Python version or platform. Cargo has features, optional dependencies, and target-specific dependencies. Gradle and Maven model scopes such as compile, runtime, and test. npm has peer dependencies whose behavior has changed even between versions of the resolver itself. RPM and Debian distinguish strong, weak, reverse, build-time, and virtual dependencies. That means that a “dependency edge” without a type and condition is semantically too weak to serve as a system of record. [10]
This is where an important algorithmic point appears: if you preserve scope, platform, feature flags, target framework, peer/weak semantics, and lockfile provenance, then many real-world questions are no longer plain reachability, but label-constrained or path-constrained reachability. The reachability survey explicitly distinguishes plain graphs from edge-labeled graphs and treats the latter as a separate class of problems with its own indexes. In practice, this means that the question “does A depend on B?” is badly posed until you specify: in what context, under which resolver, with which target, on which platform, and according to which registry snapshot. [11]
What Should Be the Source of Truth
The biggest point that the literature supports very strongly is the separation of unresolved requirements from resolved edges. The official deps.dev API has separate GetRequirements and GetDependencies endpoints; requirements are the “dependency constraints specified by the version,” while dependencies are the resolved graph resulting from those constraints. This separation is fundamental, because it forces an architecture in which manifests and clauses are stored independently of post-resolution projections. In addition, deps.dev returns graphs in which, in some systems, more than one node may appear for the same package version, and bundled dependencies may have local identity within a single artifact. That means that bare PackageVersion is not sufficient as the only type in a runtime snapshot; you also need the concept of an occurrence node or resolved instance. [12]
The canonical model closest to both the literature and production practice therefore looks not like “Neo4j nodes and edges,” but more like this IR:
Ecosystem Package PackageVersion ArtifactHash / SourceProject RequirementClause ClauseContext { scope, target_framework, os, arch, feature, extra, peer, weak, bundled, build/test/dev } ResolveRun { resolver, resolver_version, registry_snapshot, lockfile, root, environment } ResolvedOccurrence ResolvedEdge Provenance / Error / Advisory / License
This is not a literal model copied from a single publication, but a synthesis of HyperRes, Package Calculus, deps.dev API/BigQuery, and the official packaging docs. The key point is that clause and context are first-class, and that the resolved snapshot is a separate entity with its own provenance. The hash/artifact layer makes sense because deps.dev maintains both hash queries for files and the PackageVersionHashes table, which supports use cases such as vendored artifacts, SBOM enrichment, and forensics. [13]
From this perspective, the conclusion “do not start with Neo4j as the source-of-truth engine” is a well-justified inference, although it is not a sentence stated directly by any of the cited papers. The sources show that the source of truth must be able to express hyperedges, version formulae, feature unification, target-specific groups, weak and reverse dependencies, lockfile/context provenance, and the difference between an unresolved requirement and a resolved occurrence. A classical property graph can emulate all of that, but it does so unnaturally and conflates solver semantics with exploration semantics. As an exploration, debugging, and UI layer—yes. As the system of record for resolver semantics—much less convincingly. [14]
Projections for Fast Queries
Once the context has been fixed and resolution has been executed, you are in a world where classical graph representations make sense. For static snapshots, CSR and its reverse counterpart CSC remain a very strong choice: CSR provides fast outgoing scans and is natural for BFS/DFS/topological traversal, while CSC provides fast reverse-dependency and impact queries. If the system has many updates, CSR alone is too expensive to maintain; PCSR shows how to obtain much faster inserts and deletions at the cost of a constant traversal slowdown, and LSMGraph shows a more modern persistent-storage pattern: write-friendly ingest in the LSM style plus read-friendly multi-level CSR with an index across levels. If the graph is to live partly on disk or NVMe, ChunkGraph is a sensible pattern with a chunk-based representation and classified/hierarchical storage. [15]
For the reachability query layer, there is no single magic technique, but the literature gives a fairly clear direction: do not materialize the full transitive closure globally, but instead use indexes matched to the workload. The reachability survey organizes the space into tree-cover indexes, 2-hop labeling, and approximate transitive closure. One important detail: an ideal minimal 2-hop index is NP-hard to compute, so in practice one still ends up with heuristics or partial indexes. O’Reach is very pragmatic here: it declares a linear-sized reachability index and constant-time support for a large fraction of queries, with the ability to combine it with other methods. BL/minBL is a more recent example of a labeling framework aimed at reducing redundant labels. For your case, a hybrid query layer is therefore better than a single structure “for everything.” [16]
It is also worth separating two kinds of compression. SCC condensation is almost mandatory preprocessing, because even in general graphs it gives you a DAG afterward, and many reachability techniques work precisely on DAGs or derive from them. This is practically consistent with the intuition in deps.dev, where a dependency graph is “usually” a DAG, as well as with the classical O’Reach pipeline, where SCC condensation yields an acyclic backbone. By contrast, I would treat DAG compression in the STACS 2024 style as a second-stage optimization: if you observe enormous repeating subgraphs between snapshots or within a class of graphs, then structural sharing and DAG-compressed execution may be very worthwhile. The literature shows that many problems can then be computed in time dependent on the compression size rather than on the number of edges, but this is still more of a direction “after confirming the data profile” than the default project starting point. [17]
Where Ordinary Traversal Is Sufficient, and Where It Is Not
Ordinary traversal is the correct tool after resolution, or in those ecosystems that have consciously reduced the complexity of the problem. Package Managers à la Carte formally shows two paths to simplification: restricting constraints to lower bounds under a total order on versions, and removing version uniqueness. In the first case, greedy selection plus graph traversal and version sorting are sufficient; in the second, each dependency can be solved independently and the problem reduces to traversal over the dependency graph. That is exactly the moment when your “BFS/DFS/toposort only over snapshots” becomes not only practical, but formally justified. [18]
By contrast, for questions such as “does A depend on B?”, “who is affected by CVE X?”, or “what is the reverse impact of a change in package P?” pure traversal should not be the only answer. The official deps.dev BigQuery schema maintains separate tables for Dependencies, DependencyGraphEdges, and Dependents, with the dependents table explicitly computed by reversing dependencies. This supports your distinction between a canonical store and query-optimized projections: reverse impact has its own projection and its own index. My practical inference from the literature is that for cold queries, traversal over CSR/CSC on the correct snapshot is sufficient, while for hot queries, partial indexes such as O’Reach or labeling schemes such as BL/minBL are worthwhile. [19]
For security, you need one more level above that. Package-level reachability tells you that a package is present or indirectly present; it does not tell you whether the vulnerable function or method is actually reachable from your code. This is precisely where FASTEN and later work from TU Delft have the greatest value: they build or use call graphs at ecosystem scale in order to perform method-level vulnerability analysis and change impact analysis. For a modern supply-chain engine, the correct model is therefore two overlapping layers: a package/version dependency layer for resolution and impact surfacing, and a function/method reachability layer for assessing real exposure. [8]
Recommended Architecture
The shortest correct project path looks like this: per-ecosystem parsers write requirements in both the native DSL and the canonical IR; normalization maintains shared system/package/version identity, but does not lose occurrence-level and bundled/local identity; resolution runs per ResolveRun, that is, with the full context including resolver semantics, resolver version, registry snapshot, target OS/arch, scope/features/extras, and lockfile; the result of resolution is materialized as an occurrence-level snapshot; from that you build CSR for forward traversal, CSC for reverse impact, a selective reachability index for hot questions, and separate overlays for advisories, licenses, provenance, artifact hashes, and project mappings. Where you need real exploitability or change impact, you add a FASTEN-style call-graph overlay instead of trying to read everything out of the SBOM/package graph alone. [20]
My final verdict is therefore simple. I would not build this starting from a graph DB as the core. I would build it starting from a domain-specific canonical model and resolver that preserve the semantics of requirement clauses and context. I would then export one or more graph projections for traversal, reverse impact, visualization, and ad hoc exploration. This is not an anti-graph position; it is a pro-semantics position. The 2025–2026 literature very clearly supports a picture in which the core of the system is a formal resolution model, and traversal becomes cheap only after normalization, semantic disambiguation, projection, and possible indexing. [21]
